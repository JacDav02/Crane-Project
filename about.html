What is SEAP

The Science and Engineering Apprenticeship Program (SEAP) provides an opportunity for high school students to participate in research at a Department of Navy (DoN) laboratory during the summer. The goals of SEAP are to encourage participating high school students to pursue science and engineering careers, to further their education via mentoring by laboratory personnel and their participation in research, and to make them aware of DoN research and technology efforts, which can lead to employment within the DoN. SEAP is providing competitive research internships to 250 high school students this year. For more information click here.

About Us

We are a subgroup of the Naval Surface Warfar Scenter SEAP (Science and Engineering Apprenticeship Program) group based at Crane, Indiana. Our group of 9 interns combines our knowledge of science, technology, engineering and mathematics to tackle projects head on. Currently, we are working on a mother-daughter bot program, where a central mother bot is able to collect data by sending out its numerous daughter bots into the field.

Mother-Daughter Bot System

Control Station

This is the control station. This can be anything that is able to send out commands, but traditionally, we use a computer or laptop.

Mother Robot

×
The mother bot is the central robot of the system. It commands its entire fleet of daughter bots by sending them signals through its radio relay system.

Relay System

×
The relay system is composed of many small containers holding a micro-bit and a battery. The micro-bit is able to use its radio capabilities to repeat a signal from relay to relay, increasing the communication distance between a mother bot and a daughter bot.

Daughter Robot

×
The daughter bot is an EV3 Mindstorm robot which holds multiple relays on top of itself. It is able to drop these relays on the ground, thereby expanding the area where the mother's radio signal can reach. By doing so, the daughter bot can travel farther, collect more data, and scan farther surroundings.
